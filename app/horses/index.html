What Makes a Great Horse Property

Beyond acreage and zoning, here's what Donna and Jeremy evaluate on every horse property showing — things the listing sheet often doesn't tell you.

🏚️

Barn Quality & Stall Count

Steel vs. wood construction, stall size (12×12 minimum), roof condition, ventilation, and whether the barn has electricity and water plumbed to each stall.

🏟️

Arena Size & Footing

Standard arena is 100×200 ft for riding. Footing matters — decomposed granite, sand, or rubber composite all perform differently in Nevada's desert heat. Lighting is a big value-add.

⭕

Round Pen

A 50–60 ft round pen is essential for groundwork and starting young horses. Look for panels that are tall enough (5–6 ft) and securely anchored against desert winds.

🚿

Wash Rack & Tack Room

A proper wash rack with hot and cold water, good drainage, and non-slip footing. A lockable, climate-controlled tack room protects leather from extreme heat.

💧

Water Source & Rights

Nevada water law is complex. Determine whether the property is on city/county water, a private well, or has surface water rights. Horses drink 10–15 gallons daily — water cost matters.

🌾

Hay Storage

Covered hay storage protects feed from Nevada's sun and infrequent rain. Look for ventilation to prevent mold and proximity to the barn for daily feeding convenience.

🔒

Perimeter Fencing

Board, pipe rail, vinyl, or no-climb wire fencing — condition and material matter. Corner posts, gate security, and whether paddocks can be subdivided are all inspection points.

🛤️

Trail Access

Proximity to BLM land and equestrian parks dramatically increases quality of life. Properties near Henderson's trail network, Lake Mead NRA, or River Mountains Loop Trail command a premium.

📋

HOA Rules

Even horse-zoned properties can be subject to HOA restrictions. We always verify CC&Rs before writing an offer — some HOAs prohibit livestock even on A-1-zoned lots.

Nevada Horse Property — Zoning & Law Essentials

Nevada has some of the most horse-owner-friendly laws in the country. Here's what every horse property buyer and seller should understand.

A-1 Zone

Clark County Limited Agriculture — the most common horse property zoning. Typically allows horses at a density of 1–2 per acre with structures (barns, arenas, corrals). Verify the current county code with your agent before purchase, as regulations are updated periodically.

A-2 Zone

Clark County General Agriculture — permits larger-scale equestrian operations including boarding, training, and commercial riding facilities. If you plan to run a boarding or training business, A-2 or a conditional use permit is typically required.

R-E Zone

Rural Estates (Henderson / Clark County) — a suburban-rural zone that often allows a limited number of horses (commonly 1–2 per parcel, sometimes per acre). Provides a middle ground between full agricultural zoning and residential. Always verify the specific municipality's code.

NRS 40.140

Nevada Right to Farm Act — protects agricultural operations (including horse keeping) from nuisance claims by neighbors or nearby development, provided the operation predates surrounding development and follows accepted management practices. A powerful protection for established horse properties.

Water Rights

Nevada is a prior appropriation state — "first in time, first in right." Well permits, water rights certificates, and municipal water connections each have different implications for horse operations. Donna and Jeremy flag all water source questions early in the transaction so you know what you're buying.

HOA Alert

HOA CC&Rs override zoning — a property can be A-1 zoned but in an HOA that prohibits livestock. Always read the full CC&Rs, not just the MLS notes. This is one of the most common mistakes in horse property transactions. We check this before submitting any offer.

Setbacks

Clark County requires setbacks for animal structures from property lines and from existing residences on adjacent parcels. Stall locations, manure storage areas, and arenas must all comply. A pre-purchase survey helps confirm existing improvements are code-compliant.

Boarding / Training

Commercial equestrian operations (boarding, training, lessons, breeding) typically require a business license and may require a conditional use permit (CUP) from Clark County or the applicable municipality. We connect buyers planning commercial operations with the right permit consultants early.

Horse Property FAQs

What Clark County zoning allows horses?

A-1 (Limited Agriculture) and A-2 (General Agriculture) are the primary zones. Some R-E (Rural Estates) designations in Henderson also allow limited horse keeping. Always verify with the county planning department and your agent before purchasing.

Does Nevada protect horse property owners from neighbor complaints?

Yes. Nevada's Right to Farm Act (NRS 40.140) shields agricultural operations — including horse keeping — from nuisance lawsuits by neighbors or new developments, as long as the operation predates surrounding development and follows accepted management practices.

Can an HOA restrict horses even on A-1 zoned land?

Yes, and this is critical. HOA CC&Rs can and do override county zoning in some developments. A property can be A-1 zoned and still be in an HOA that prohibits livestock. Donna and Jeremy always verify CC&Rs before writing an offer — this is a known pitfall in horse property transactions.

What are the best horse property areas near Henderson?

East Henderson (Pittman corridor), Boulder City outskirts, and North Las Vegas A-1 zones are the primary areas. Each has different price points and property sizes. Donna and Jeremy know each pocket well and can match your needs — stall count, arena size, proximity to trails — to the right area.

How does well water work on Nevada horse properties?

Nevada is a prior appropriation water state. Private wells require a permit from the Nevada Division of Water Resources. Well capacity, depth, recovery rate, and water rights certificates are all things we verify during due diligence. Horses drink 10–15 gallons per day — water availability is not negotiable.

Can I run a horse boarding or training business from a residential property?

Typically, commercial operations (boarding for pay, group lessons, training clients' horses) require A-2 zoning and/or a Conditional Use Permit (CUP) plus a business license. We connect buyers planning commercial operations with the appropriate permitting consultants as part of our buyer advisory process.

What should I budget for horse property maintenance in Nevada?

Desert maintenance is unique: UV-degraded fencing and roofing, high water costs (sometimes $200–$500/month for horses on city water), dust control in the arena, and summer heat management for horses and hay storage. Donna and Jeremy give buyers a realistic picture of ongoing costs, not just purchase price.
